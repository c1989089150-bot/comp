Interview 2:
Age: 20, Degree: Computer Science, International student, Year 2

Speaker 1: Can you tell me a bit about how supported you feel when making course selection decisions?
Speaker 2: I feel pretty supported when choosing courses, but most of the help comes from talking to academic advisors, upper classmates, and occasionally my professors. Yeah.
Speaker 1: Can you tell me about how you usually check prerequisites or course information before enrolling?
Speaker 2: Before enrolling, I usually start by opening the university's course catalog and searching for the class I want. I scroll down to find prerequisites, double-check if I’m taking the required courses or have earned the necessary credits, and also look at the course descriptions. Yeah.
Speaker 1: OK, imagine a tool where you could select a course and immediately see its prerequisites and key details. How would you expect to use something like that?
Speaker 2: If there was a tool where I could just pick a course and instantly see the prerequisites and key details, I’d use it every time I browse potential classes.
Speaker 1: At what point in your course selection process would a tool like this be most useful to you?
Speaker 2: This tool would be most useful at the very beginning of course planning, when I'm still brainstorming and building my schedule. Yeah.
Speaker 1: And what part of a tool like this would be most valuable to you? What other features would you want it to include?
Speaker 2: The most valuable part would definitely be clear and intuitive prerequisite checks, so I don’t accidentally sign up for a class I can’t take. Yeah.
Speaker 1: So, what might stop you from using a tool like this?
Speaker 2: What would stop me from using it is if the information is outdated or inaccurate. I also wouldn’t use it if the interface is complicated or if it requires too many logins. Yeah, that’s it.
Speaker 1: What would make you trust a website or tool to help with course selection?
Speaker 2: OK, I’d choose a tool if it has official recommendations or is backed by my university, if the information is updated regularly, and if other students say it’s reliable. Yeah.
Speaker 1: Last question: What would make a tool like this more useful than the other course information resources currently available to you?
Speaker 2: This tool would be better than current resources because everything is in one place. Right now, I have to use the course catalog, academic advising portals, professor profiles, and advisor emails—they’re all spread out. Yeah. OK, thank you very much.
