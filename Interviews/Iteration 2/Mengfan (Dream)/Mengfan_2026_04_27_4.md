Interview 4:
Age: 20, Degree: Enginnering, International student, Year 2

Speaker 1:
How supported do you feel when making course selection decisions?
Speaker 2:
Pretty supported overall. Since I’m in Engineering, most courses are compulsory, so the university gives me a clear list of what to take. But when it comes to actual difficulty or workload, the website doesn’t really help. I usually just ask friends who’ve taken the course before.

Speaker 1:
How do you usually decide if a course is too hard or manageable?
Speaker 2:
Mostly by asking friends or seniors. Sometimes I just guess based on the course level, like the course code or year level, but it’s not very reliable.

Speaker 1:
Can you walk me through how you usually check prerequisites or course information before enrolling?
Speaker 2:
I go to the UQ website, find my degree under the Study page, and there’s a “What You Will Study” section with the program structure. Then I click into individual courses to check prerequisites and descriptions. It works, but the information is pretty scattered — I have to jump between a lot of pages.

Speaker 1:
Like you mentioned, what’s the most frustrating part about switching between different pages?
Speaker 2:
Probably losing track of information. Sometimes I forget which page had what, and I have to go back and check again.

Speaker 1:
Imagine a tool where you could select a course and immediately see its prerequisites and key details — how would you expect to use something like that?
Speaker 2:
I’d use it for planning. Instead of opening lots of tabs, I could just search a course and see everything in one place. I’d also use it to compare courses side by side. It would be really helpful if it could warn me when I haven’t completed a prerequisite yet.

Speaker 1:
At what point in your course selection process would a tool like this be most useful?
Speaker 2:
Definitely before the semester starts, when I’m deciding what to register for. That’s when I need information the fastest, and right now it’s quite stressful jumping between pages and trying to check everything at once.

Speaker 1:
What part of a tool like this would be most valuable, and what other features would you want?
Speaker 2:
The most valuable part is having everything in one place — like prerequisites, workload, and assessments all on one page. I’d also want student reviews, a workload indicator, an assessment summary, automatic course recommendations based on my major, and a semester planning table that checks if my plan makes sense.

Speaker 1:
Which of these features would be the most important to you?
Speaker 2:
Probably workload and student reviews, because that’s the kind of information I can’t really find on the official website.

Speaker 1:
What might stop you from using a tool like this?
Speaker 2:
If the information is inaccurate or outdated, like wrong prerequisites, that would be a real problem. Also, if it’s not actually faster than the UQ website, there’s no point. And if the design is cluttered or confusing, I’d probably stop using it.

Speaker 1:
What would make you trust a tool like this?
Speaker 2:
Seeing real UQ students recommending it, like on social media or in group chats. It would also help if it’s officially connected to UQ or recommended by a lecturer. Basically, student endorsement plus some official backing.

Speaker 1:
What would make this tool more useful than existing resources?
Speaker 2:
Having everything in one place already makes it better than the current experience. But what would really make it stand out is features the UQ website doesn’t have, like student reviews, workload indicators, and a visual semester planning table that can automatically check your plan. That’s something you can’t really do anywhere else right now.
