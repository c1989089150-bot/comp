# Learning Card 1
## Hypothesis 
We believe the lack of filtered elective options and clear course information causes students to waste time, make incorrect choices, and feel unsupported.

## Observation 
We noticed in the interviews that students often spend a significant amount of time browsing different platforms to find elective courses. Many reported that course information is scattered and unclear, making it difficult to compare options efficiently.
Students also expressed concerns about accidentally selecting ineligible courses due to unclear prerequisite information. In addition, while some support is available (e.g., teachers or advisors), students still feel uncertain when making final decisions.

## Conclusion 
The hypothesis is partially validated. While students do feel supported to some extent, they still experience inefficiencies and uncertainty due to fragmented information and lack of filtering tools. The main issue is not the absence of support, but the lack of structured and accessible course information.

## Next Step
We should design a prototype that includes: Filtered elective options (e.g., by major, difficulty, semester); Clear prerequisite visualization; Centralized course information
Then test whether this reduces decision time and improves confidence.

# Learning Card 2
## Hypothesis 
We believe manual course enrollment increases the risk of missing core courses or enrolling in conflicting classes.

## Observation 
From the interviews, some students reported worrying about missing important courses, especially those only offered in specific semesters. However, actual cases of missing enrollment or timetable conflicts were less frequent than expected.
Instead, a more common issue was uncertainty about whether a course aligns with their future career goals. Students often rely on personal judgment, advice from peers, or limited online descriptions when making decisions.

## Conclusion 
The hypothesis is not strongly supported. While risks exist, they are not the primary pain point. The more significant issue is lack of guidance on course relevance to career goals, rather than technical enrollment errors.

## Next Step
Shift focus from enrollment automation to decision support, such as: Showing how courses relate to career paths; Providing recommendations based on goals; Including reviews or outcomes from past students

# Learning Card 3
## Hypothesis 
We believe international students face additional confusion due to unfamiliarity with the university’s course registration system.

## Observation 
We noticed that international students frequently mentioned difficulties navigating the course registration system. Common issues included unfamiliar terminology, unclear system structure, and lack of guidance on how to plan courses across semesters.
Some students also reported relying heavily on external help (friends, seniors, or agents), indicating that the system itself does not provide sufficient clarity.

## Conclusion 
The hypothesis is validated. International students experience more confusion compared to domestic students, primarily due to unfamiliar systems and lack of intuitive guidance.

## Next Step
We should design features specifically targeting international students, such as:
Step-by-step onboarding for course selection
Simplified interface with clearer terminology
Visual study plan (timeline across semesters)
Contextual help or examples

# Learning Card 4
## Hypothesis
We believe students find course information scattered across multiple platforms, which makes it time-consuming and frustrating to compare courses and make decisions.

## Observation
Students consistently described a fragmented workflow when searching for course information. They reported opening multiple platforms (e.g. handbook, LMS, timetable systems, and unofficial sources) to gather basic details such as prerequisites, course descriptions, and scheduling. This process often led to confusion, duplicated effort, and difficulty comparing courses side by side. Many students also mentioned that important information is not presented in a consistent format across platforms.

## Conclusion
The hypothesis is strongly validated. Information fragmentation is a major usability barrier that significantly reduces efficiency in course selection.

## Next Step
We should prioritise building a unified course information system that:
Aggregates all course-related data into one platform
Standardises course presentation format for easier comparison
Reduces the need to switch between multiple systems
We should also test whether centralisation improves decision speed and reduces perceived cognitive load.

# Learning Card 5
## Hypothesis
We believe students will value a centralized tool that integrates all course-related information and presents it clearly, making it more useful than existing resources.

## Observation
Students consistently expressed strong preference for a single, centralised platform that consolidates course information. They highlighted that current systems are inefficient, repetitive, and difficult to navigate. Many participants stated that they would prefer a tool that allows them to compare courses directly, rather than manually collecting information from multiple sources. Features such as clarity, time-saving, and structured comparison were repeatedly mentioned as high-value needs.

## Conclusion
The hypothesis is validated. There is strong demand for a centralized, integrated course planning tool that simplifies decision-making and reduces information overload.

## Next Step
We should prioritise MVP development around:
Centralised course dashboard
Side-by-side course comparison
Smart filtering and search functions
Integration of prerequisites, timetable, and course outcomes
Future testing should measure whether centralisation reduces decision time and increases confidence in course selection.
